Biography

Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon, known to the world as Maimonides or by the Hebrew acronym RamBam, was born in 1138 in Córdoba, Spain, during the golden age of Jewish culture in the Iberian Peninsula. His father, Rabbi Maimon, was a distinguished judge and scholar who instilled in the young Moses a deep love of learning and devotion to Jewish law.

When Maimonides was thirteen, the Almohad conquest of Córdoba forced his family to flee. For nearly a decade, they wandered through Spain and eventually settled in Fez, Morocco, where Maimonides continued his intensive studies in Torah, philosophy, and medicine. In 1165, the family moved to the Land of Israel before finally settling in Fustat (Old Cairo), Egypt.

In Egypt, Maimonides became the leader of the Jewish community and rose to prominence as a physician, eventually becoming the personal doctor to the Sultan Saladin and his royal court. Despite his demanding medical practice, which occupied much of his day, he devoted his nights to writing and to answering legal queries from Jewish communities across the world. He passed away in 1204 and was buried in Tiberias, where his tomb remains a site of pilgrimage to this day.

Major Works

Mishneh Torah

משנה תורה

A comprehensive codification of all Jewish law, organized into fourteen books. This monumental work systematically presents the entire scope of halakha (Jewish religious law), making it accessible to scholars and laypeople alike. It remains one of the most influential works in Jewish legal literature.

Guide for the Perplexed

מורה נבוכים

Maimonides' philosophical masterpiece, written in Judeo-Arabic for intellectuals struggling to reconcile religious faith with Aristotelian philosophy. The work addresses profound questions about God's nature, prophecy, divine providence, and the reasons behind the commandments.

Commentary on the Mishnah

פירוש המשנה

Written over ten years, this Arabic commentary explains the Mishnah's legal and ethical teachings. Its introduction includes the famous formulation of the Thirteen Principles of Faith, which became a cornerstone of Jewish belief and is recited in many communities to this day.

The Thirteen Principles of Faith

Maimonides formulated these fundamental beliefs as the essential tenets of Judaism, expressing the core theological concepts that define Jewish faith.

  1. 1 Existence of God: God exists and is the cause of all existence. All beings depend on Him, but He depends on nothing.
  2. 2 Unity of God: God is absolutely one. His unity is unlike any other unity that exists. He is not divisible or composite in any way.
  3. 3 Incorporeality of God: God has no body and no physical form. Any biblical descriptions of God in physical terms are metaphorical.
  4. 4 Eternity of God: God is eternal, the first and the last. He existed before all things and will exist forever.
  5. 5 Worship of God Alone: It is proper to worship God alone and no other being. No intermediaries should be called upon.
  6. 6 Prophecy: God communicates with humanity through prophets, chosen individuals of great wisdom and moral perfection.
  7. 7 Supremacy of Moses: The prophecy of Moses was unique and superior to all other prophets, before and after him.
  8. 8 Divine Origin of Torah: The entire Torah was given by God to Moses. It is divine in origin, both the Written and Oral Torah.
  9. 9 Immutability of Torah: The Torah will never be changed or replaced. No other law will ever come from God.
  10. 10 Divine Omniscience: God knows all the deeds and thoughts of human beings. Nothing is hidden from His knowledge.
  11. 11 Reward and Punishment: God rewards those who keep His commandments and punishes those who transgress them.
  12. 12 Coming of the Messiah: The Messiah will come, and though he may tarry, we must await his coming every day.
  13. 13 Resurrection of the Dead: The dead will be brought back to life when God wills it.

Enduring Legacy

⚖️

Jewish Law

The Mishneh Torah revolutionized the study of halakha, organizing centuries of legal discussion into a clear, systematic code. It remains a primary reference for Jewish law and continues to shape rabbinic decisions worldwide.

📚

Philosophy

Maimonides bridged the gap between faith and reason, influencing not only Jewish thought but also Christian and Islamic philosophy. Thinkers like Thomas Aquinas and Leibniz drew upon his ideas.

⚕️

Medicine

His medical writings, emphasizing preventive care and holistic health, were studied for centuries. His approach to medicine as both art and science anticipated modern medical ethics.
